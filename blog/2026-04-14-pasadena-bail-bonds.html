Where Do Pasadena Arrests Go?

When Pasadena Police Department officers make an arrest, the defendant is transported to the Pasadena Police Department Jail at 207 N Garfield Avenue, Pasadena, CA 91101 (phone: 626-744-4241). This is a city-operated Type I detention facility — meaning it holds defendants for short periods, typically less than 96 hours, while booking is completed and bail is posted or other arrangements are made.

The Pasadena PD Jail is the first stop for the vast majority of arrests made within Pasadena city limits. This includes DUI stops, domestic violence calls, drug possession arrests, assault charges, and warrant arrests. Officers book the defendant on-site: fingerprints, photographs, property inventory, and medical screening all happen at 207 N Garfield.

For serious felony charges — or when the city jail is at capacity — defendants are transferred into the custody of the Los Angeles County Sheriff's Department (LASD). The two main LASD intake facilities are:

The Booking Process at Pasadena PD Jail — Step by Step

The booking process at the Pasadena Police Department Jail follows California law. Here is what happens from the moment of arrest to the point where bail can be posted:

Arrival
Transport and Property Inventory
Officers transport the defendant to 207 N Garfield Ave. Personal belongings — phone, wallet, keys, jewelry — are inventoried, bagged, and logged. The defendant receives a receipt for their property, which is returned at release.
Identity
ID Verification and Warrant Search
Officers confirm the defendant's identity and run their name through state and federal criminal databases. Any outstanding warrants from other jurisdictions — including LA County, other California counties, or federal warrants — are flagged here. An active warrant can place a hold that complicates release even after bail is posted.
Biometrics
Fingerprints and Booking Photo
Electronic fingerprints (Live Scan) are submitted to the California Department of Justice and the FBI. A booking photograph is taken. Criminal history confirmed through this process can affect the bail amount assigned or whether the judge adjusts bail at arraignment.
Medical
Health and Mental Health Screening
California law requires a health screening for every booked individual. Defendants with immediate medical needs may be transported to a hospital before booking is completed — which can extend the timeline before bail can be posted.
Bail Set
Bail Amount Assigned from Schedule
Bail is set according to the Los Angeles County bail schedule for the specific charges filed. This is the amount a bondsman works from. A bail bond can be posted as soon as this step is complete — California imposes no mandatory waiting period after booking is finished.

Misdemeanor bookings at Pasadena PD Jail typically take 2 to 6 hours from the time of arrival. Felony bookings can run longer — 6 to 24 hours — particularly if a transfer to an LASD facility is required. The moment booking is complete and bail is assigned, a bondsman can post the bond.

The Superior Court of California, County of Los Angeles publishes its Felony and Misdemeanor Bail Schedules annually, effective January 1. These amounts are assigned at booking based on the charges filed. A judge may adjust bail upward or downward at arraignment based on criminal history, flight risk, community ties, and the nature of the offense. Under California Penal Code § 825, arraignment must occur within 48 hours of arrest, excluding Sundays and court holidays. Posting bail before arraignment locks in the scheduled amount — waiting risks a higher amount or a denial under CA Constitution Article I, Section 12 for certain violent offenses.

Note on LA County's Pre-Arraignment Release Protocol (PARP): Effective January 2026, Los Angeles County uses a risk-assessment tool for misdemeanor defendants to determine pre-arraignment release without bail. Many non-violent misdemeanor defendants are now released without cash bail. However, domestic violence charges (PC 273.5, PC 243(e)(1)), violent felonies, and defendants with active protective orders are specifically excluded from PARP — those cases still require a traditional bail bond.

Pasadena Courthouse and What Happens After Arrest

Once a defendant is booked at Pasadena PD Jail and a bond is posted, the next milestone is arraignment. All Pasadena criminal cases are heard at the Pasadena Courthouse, Northeast District, at 300 E Walnut Street, Pasadena, CA 91101 (phone: 626-356-5689). The courthouse operates Monday through Friday, 8:30am to 4:30pm and handles criminal, civil, family law, and juvenile matters for the Northeast District of Los Angeles County.

Under California Penal Code § 825, the defendant must be brought before a judge within 48 hours of arrest, not counting Sundays or court holidays. At arraignment, the judge:

  • Formally reads the charges
  • Takes the defendant's plea (typically not guilty at this stage)
  • Reviews and potentially adjusts the bail amount set at booking
  • Issues any protective orders — in domestic violence cases, a criminal protective order under PC 136.2 is standard and requires the defendant to stay away from the alleged victim
  • Sets the next court date (preliminary hearing for felonies, pre-trial conference for misdemeanors)

If bail was posted before arraignment, the defendant attends their arraignment from home — they are not re-arrested unless the judge revokes bail. This is one of the most important reasons to call a bondsman immediately after learning of an arrest: posting bail before arraignment means your loved one walks into court standing beside their attorney instead of being transported from a jail cell.

Domestic Violence Arrests in Pasadena — What Families Need to Know

Domestic violence calls are one of the most frequent types of arrests in Pasadena. Under California law, when officers respond to a DV call and observe evidence of injury or have probable cause, an arrest is mandatory. The arresting officers do not have discretion — they must take someone into custody.

The most common DV charges in Pasadena:

  • Penal Code 273.5 — Corporal Injury on Spouse or Cohabitant: This is a felony. Any visible injury caused to a spouse, former spouse, cohabitant, or parent of the defendant's child can support this charge. The 2026 LA County bail schedule sets this at $50,000. With a prior conviction, bail jumps to $100,000.
  • Penal Code 243(e)(1) — Domestic Battery: This is a misdemeanor covering battery against a spouse, cohabitant, or intimate partner when no visible injury results. Bail is typically around $10,000, though PARP may apply for first-time defendants with no active orders.
  • Penal Code 136.2 — Criminal Protective Order: This is not a bail hold — this is a court order issued at arraignment. It typically requires the defendant to stay away from the alleged victim and have no contact. Violating a PC 136.2 order is a separate criminal charge. Families who call us asking why their loved one "can't come home" after a DV arrest often confuse this order with a bail hold. The bail was already posted — the protective order is a separate restriction.
  • Penal Code 422 — Criminal Threats: Often charged alongside DV incidents. Sending threatening messages, voicemails, or making verbal threats causing sustained fear is a wobbler — can be filed as misdemeanor or felony. Felony bail set at $50,000.
  • Penal Code 646.9 — Stalking: When a DV situation escalates over time to include a pattern of following, contacting, or surveilling the alleged victim in a way that causes fear. Felony bail can reach $150,000.

Critical fact for Pasadena DV arrests: California law does NOT impose a mandatory 72-hour hold for domestic violence cases. This is a persistent myth. There is no statute requiring a defendant to remain in custody for 72 hours. A bail bond can be posted as soon as booking is complete and bail is set per the county schedule — which typically happens within 2 to 8 hours of the arrest. Do not wait.

The one restriction specific to DV cases: the defendant may be required to wait until they see a judge at arraignment (within 48 hours) before being released in certain circumstances. This is a judicial determination, not an automatic rule. In practice, most Pasadena DV defendants are eligible for bail immediately after booking unless there is a history of serious prior violence or an active court order already in place.
